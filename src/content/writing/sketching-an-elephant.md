## i.

Futurelessness

- we have lost a sense of the future, leaving us only the ever changing-present and the abstract 'always'
- this is compounded by LMs, accelerating the ability to recombine the past far past human control
- this is important; not just because we clearly face crisis in our near future which requires a culture which can dream how the world could be transformed, but because a common belief in the future is an institution of any communal collaboration.

Capitalist Realism [^capitalist-realism]

"it is now impossible to even _imagine_ a coherent alternative to \[capitalism\]"
"How long can a culture persist without the new?"
"Eliot's claim was that the exhausation of the future does not even leave us with the past... A culture that is merely preserved is no culture at all."
"a deeper, far more pervasive, sense of exhaustion, of cultural and political sterility."

Marx, quoted p49 Radical Abundance[^radical-abundance], "the advance of capitalist production develops a working class which by education, tradition and habit looks upon the requirements of that mode of production as self-evident natural laws".

The passing of Mark Fisher in 2014(?) means we will never hear his diagnosis of the cultural effects of Large Language Models. But his writing over 15 years ago is so prescient that it can still be applied. In today's "AI", we are literally creating a technology which infinitely recombines dead culture. Fisher quotes Frederick(?) Jameson, who describes "a world in which stylistic innovation is no longer possible, \[where\] all that is left is to imitate dead styles, to speak through the masks and with the voices of the styles in the imaginary museum." This was written in (check); it resonated in 2009 when Fisher wrote Capitalist Realism; and how can it not resonate even more today, as our whole economy reorients to create machines to hyperscale 'speak\[ing\] through the masks'.

However impressive the technology, LMs aren't especially novel in their qualitative effects. We already lived in a society of infinite sequels, remakes, references, trends, of banal, empty, padded language, of procedure and performance without a clear purpose. It's already becoming clear that using LLMs will atrophy our ability to create anything without them. But why would this give us any pause, when we have so deeply internalised that nothing new is possible anyway?

Of course, there are still many thinkers proposing individual visions of the future - with absolutely no numbers to back this up, I would feel confident in guessing that more manifestos are written now than at any point in history. But I cannot see evidence of our collective, cultural imagination being energised by a world that might be. Instead, the prevailing attitude seems to be a pre-emptive, resigned cynicism, what Mark Fisher coins as capitalist realism. This attitude says that the world may not be perfect, but dreaming of better is a path to disappointment at best, and destruction at worst. "The 'realism' here is analogous to the deflationary perspective of a depressive who believes that any positive state, any hope, is a dangerous illusion." [^capitalist-realism] Common sense dictates that history ended in 1991, and there's nothing left to do but scroll.

It hardly needs saying that in a time so beset by crisis, a collective inability to imagine how we might live differently is an existentially dangerous condition. "In emergencies, systems optimise to not break today"[softwank]; and in a culture which only has an endless, amnesiac 'now', this means an eternal state of almost-emergency, an endlessly rising cultural tension, a [Shepard under-tone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shepard_tone) to our lives. The psyche of depression again feels apt - the dull dread of accumulating failure, only getting more insurmountable each day you defer confronting it. Perhaps you're daring the dam to break. Maybe it would be a relief for the future to crash into you.

Ivan Krastev on [The Weekly Show with Jon Stewart][weekly-show].

Futurelessness is not just an instrumental risk to our ability to combat societal crisis; it begets a crisis of its own. Ivan Krastev (on [The Weekly Show with Jon Stewart][weekly-show], of all places) that we have an increasingnumber of "--What makes me really nervous about the world in which we are living is that you have basically-- people who fear the future... And when you fear the future, you believe that, if there is going to be a fight, better fight today. Because tomorrow, we're going to be weaker."

He continues, "This deficit of the future is a huge problem for democracy. Because the future is one of the invisible institutions of democracy. If you don't believe in the future, democracy cannot function." Thanks to the future, we can accept compromise, because there will be more chances to have our way. But if the music is stopping, if our now might be forever, or at least whatever's left, then there is no time for compromise.

## ii.

Luddites

The Luddites. Finding the language for a new moment.

Can pull on the thread of the lost future, as time repeats this story again.

- the First industrial revolution was defined by technologies which contested the need for physical, manual skill
- We now face a new revolution, which has such an eerie _resonance_ with the forces the Luddites faced that one has to identify it as part of one greater story.
- in this revolution, the technologies being unleashed on society contest the need for mental, inventive skill, completing the subsumption of human value into mechanised forces of production
- expound the mapping between these two moments of rupture:
  - mills | data centres
  - spinners, weavers and croppers | writers, designers, educators
  - compensation for the loss of employment and opportunity, and for the extraction of skilled work to create the means of its replacement.
  - the clear and reasonable alternative; that these technologies be made commonly available and accessible, so that they can lighten the burdens of those doing the work they transform, rather than destroying those peoples' lives to the benefit of a tiny oligarchy.
- Bring back the rattle in the engine, that now resonates across time, drives new vibrations in accord that amplify the tremors. Like a glass as the right pitch is reached, this resonance is going to drive those tremors up and up until they shake the engine apart; the question is, what new history will we build out of this rupture?
  Once again there is a revolution of industry, a wave of technology that stands to upend our social relations and usher in a new world. Once again, the established powers who wield that technology are doing so with cruel, brutal disregard for their fellow humans.

The parallel is so direct that it almost feels tasteless to spell out. Technological developments in the past generation created a whole new sphere of employment

Crucial to emphasise that this was not technophobic, regressive, conservative. We gravitate to reading it this way today, because we've never lived in times where what the future could be has been open to discussion. The Luddites absolutely understood what the new machines could do, and were more than willing to entertain a future transformed by them. When they swung the hammer, it was not to destroy progress _per se_, but to destroy the vision of the future being manifested by the owners of those machines.

From 'Capital is Dead' [^capital-is-dead]

p51: "It is possible to hold onto a feeling about what it's like to make elegance appear that wasn't there before with a technique for transforming information, and to do it on one's own time, with one's own goals and objectives".

"It is all but inconceivable now that there could be an open-ended, playful, experimental approach to making the new appear out of the old in techniques of information that would not be entirely contained within the commodification and control of the information vector."

p59: "In vulgar terms: the capitalist class eats our bodies; the vectoralist class eats our brains". Reference to articles about "AI Fry" (linked by Linnea in Teams) etc.

## iii.

The indefinite article. Radical multiplicity.

I hope everyone has had the experience of dizziness from thinking about infinity, really thinking about what it could mean. All the constituent parts are intuitive and logical; we understand what it means to take some number of things, and add one more to the collection. But then the axioms grow wings while you aren't looking. What can it mean that there is a number beyond all others, that you could count the atoms in the universe, and then continue in steps of that size as if each counted as one, and be no closer to the goal? That there are infinities even greater than this, indeed an infinity of them?

When I try to parse the entangled cause and effect that is the news, I often get the same sense of conceptual vertigo, a wrenching in my gut like rollercoasters and nightmares. It's claustrophobia and agoraphobia at once, feeling completely daunted by how impossible it is to actually understand all of what has happened. Phenomena are spread and entangled across space and time, so that everything admits multiple perspectives depending on the framing of the viewer. Concepts like Polycrisis (Adam Tooze? Or popularised by him from another theorist?) illustrate this attempt to point at the centre of something defined by the absence of one.

Mathematics has a truly beautiful response to this vertigo, a kind of radical acceptance. We see that our axioms have created a consequence that we can define but cannot understand. But if we can define its essence, we can name the thing that definition points to; we capture infinity as Aleph-null, as omega, an implicit reference to something our minds cannot explicitly contain. And having found the language for the concept, it immediately becomes tame. It is subject to our axioms, now suitably extended, and we can deduce new consequences from it. The towering edifice of maths has been built step by step in this way, by facing the limits of what we can understand, and finding the right abstraction to capture that next infinity.

Unfortunately, it is hubris to believe (although many still do) that the human experience can be resolved with the same axiomatic precision. Finding the language with which to speak about things newly born from history is an art which has belonged to very few, and their names are etched in history by the achievement. Guy Debord expressed the longing to find those words in his _Memoires_: "I wanted to speak the beautiful language of my century".

Our new world, brought forth by a historical confluence of neoliberalism and network technology, has not yet found the great mind who can teach us that beautiful language. We have come as far as our old concepts can take us, but the systems those concepts built have gone forward without us. We do not have the senses required to perceive the whole shape of our new world. We can only perceive it through touch, through local observation, and so we can only know it by sketches.

Just as Marx prophesied, all that was solid has melted into air. That same atomising force sublimates any new solids which attempt to form. So we must learn to think with air, to survive on it; there will be no more ideas which we can eat and feel full, just a continuous exchange of breath.

Maybe with the sense that we lack, we would perceive the endless act of sketching itself as the perfect representation of that form; a infinitely looping GIF, a list ending in an ellipsis.

"A hacker manifesto: Not the only manifesto, as it is in the nature of the hacker to differ from others, to differ even from oneself, over time."

Today, Luddites must insist, everywhere, locally and concretely, that new information technologies will not be introduced in a manner that enriches the new mills.

"We no longer know what socialism is, or how to get there, and yet it remains the goal." -Deng Xiaoping, [^capital-is-dead] chapter 5 epigraph.

"The building rage that must remain immpotent because it can have no legitimate object, since... there is no-one who knows, and no-one who could do anything even if they \[did\]." [^capitalist-realism]

Later, on memory loss and Jason Bourne, "In conditions where realities and identities are upgraded like software..."[^capitalist-realism]

An interesting aspect of the story of the Luddites is that it does not involve any single concept of "Luddism", It's never really required - the ethos seems to emergy locally, naturally, specific to each town and city and yet clearly coherent across the wider place and time.

Fisher says that market Stalinism "... can only emergy in a late capitalist culture **in which images acquire autonomous force**". The temptation to coin a 'neo-Luddism' is exactly this cultural force - everything today is an -ism, a movement, a school of thought, an aesthetic. And once the idea is reified as a thing in its own right, it can be separated from the community of actions and practices that gave rise to it. Thinkpieces and opinion columns can can pick apart the -ism without any specifics of the individual beliefs, begin horse-trading about its electoral merits, its cultural legitimacy, what it can teach us about Gen Z or B2B sales. So perhaps the way to break out, the missing "language of our century", is to embrace multiplicity, to defy reification; to accept definition only as a centreless collection, a multitude of situated actions. We do not need a Luddism. We need Luddites.

## iv.

An intervention. Popular protagonism [^radical-abundance]. A commons of visions for resistance.

China Mieville's 'Iron Council' is a very direct exploration of post-industrial revolution. As with everything he writes, every concept introduced feels designed to unsettle you, to reject simplistic re-use of fantasy tropes that would comfort the reader. And in this book, the third in a trilogy which has already established a richly textured fantasy world, he introduces and dwells on the mechanics of creating golems.

"Golemetry is matter made to view itself anew, given a command that organises it, a task". "This act of pointing is at least as much assertion and persuasion as observation. We see structure, and in pointing it out we see mechanisms and grasp them, and we twist." The creation of golems is an _intervention_ in the ordinary course of the universe - nothing new is created, merely transformed by telling a story about what it already is. In golemetry, Mieville creates a magical concept that perfectly illuminates his human themes, that uses fictional juxtaposition to speak more clearly than would be possible in pure theory. It's writing that makes me want to stand up and applaud.

Again from capitalist realism: "The required subject - a collective subject - does not exist, yet the crisis... demands that it be constructed."

From Radical Abundance on popular protagonism, p49: "For popular protagonism to develop..., two things must happen simultaneously.

- First, the associated producers must learn through acting collectively they have the power to change the world, which is to say they must become conscious of themselves as a collective political subject.
- Second, the associated producers must build institutional forms that empower them to act in their collective interest both at the site of production and in broader practies of social reproduction."

A pre-manifesto is a request, a call, a plea for such a movement to come into being. Or perhaps, to realise that it is already in being, to animate what already exists with a story about what it is.

"Which leaves us with the paradoxical and provocative thought that any theory in which the present appears as in essence the same as the past of Marx's time, only different in a few matters of appearance, can't really be a "Marxist" one, as such fidelity is necessarily a betrayal of his achievement."
